The Ancient History of Roads

Like many things developed over the course of thousands of years, roads have a very vibrant and storied past. The paved streets and highways of today are a far cry from the networks of dirt roads constructed in ancient civilizations, but they have all served the same ultimate purpose: to move people and goods from one place to another. Let’s take a look at the history of roads, starting from the Stone Age and ending in current day.

~5000 BC: The Ridgeway

Britain’s oldest road, known as The Ridgeway, is a historically significant trade route known for its ease of travel and commanding view. As opposed to paved roads such as those later developed in Greece, The Ridgeway is a naturally-occurring fixture of the environment and thus required no human maintenance. In 1973, it was declared a National Trail, passing through many historic landmarks in southern England.

~2500 BC: Paved roads

The creation of the wheel established the need for sturdier roads. The world’s oldest known paved road could be found in Egypt, with a width of six and a half feet and running for 43 miles (69 km) through desert terrain southwest of modern Cairo. Improvements in metallurgy also led to the construction of paved roads in other parts of the world, most notably in Greece and throughout the Middle East.

~500 BC: The Royal Road

The Dicle Bridge, a historic bridge in Eastern Turkey. Photo by Dûrzan cîrano

Likely one of the most ambitious road networks of ancient times, the Royal Road was developed within the first Persian Empire in order to facilitate rapid communications. In its entirety, the Royal Road stretched for an impressive 1,677 miles (2,699 km), and was primarily used by postmen.

500 BC - 400 AD: Roman roads

An ancient road in Pompeii. Photo by Paul Vlaar

Covering a whopping total of about 250,000 miles (400,000 km), the Roman road system was unarguably the most extensive system of transportation during the turn of the century. Much of this system comprised of well-engineered highways, composed of multiple layers for maximum durability. These roads contained modern amenities such as drainage ditches and footpaths, and some of these roads are still in use today.

8th century AD: Tar-paved roads

A tar-paved road. Probably not in Baghdad.

Believe it or not, tar-paved roads could be found as early as 1,200 years ago in the oil-rich city of Baghdad. Following this time period, there was little innovation in the field of road-building until the 18th century.

14th century - 17th century: Renaissance roads

One of George Wade’s bridges in Scotland.

During the Renaissance time period, many new roads were built. Oftentimes these roads were based on Roman designs, and some were very poorly constructed. In the early 1700s, for example, General George Wade built 250 miles (400 km) of roads and bridges in the Scottish Highlands, but these roads were plagued with problems such as steep grades, poor drainage, and rough surfaces.

18th century: Turnpikes

_The Great North Road), an early turnpike._

The 1700s perhaps marked the very beginning of the period of modern road development and civil engineering. During this time, such developments could primarily be found in the modern-day United Kingdom. Road technologies began slowly advancing, and variables such as geographical features and weather conditions were taken into consideration. Later on in this time period, turnpikes were also developed in the United States, often paralleling or replacing some of the heavily-traveled existing routes.

19th century: New techniques

Gordon Road, originally constructed in 19th century Fareham, England. Photo by Colin Babb

During the Industrial Revolution, revolutionary new methods for highway development were pioneered in Britain. Beginning in the 19th century, smooth surfaces and drainage systems were heavily prioritized. At this point in time, such roads were usually tolled. New concepts spread quickly throughout Europe, and eventually around the world.

20th century - present: Modern roads

_A remnant of the Long Island Motor Parkway, the first road designed solely for automotive use._ Photo by Nowa at English Wikipedia

In the late 1800s, roads outside of cities were usually made of either gravel or dirt. This time period marked the beginning of the Good Roads Movement in the United States, which aimed to improve transportation by paving these roads. In 1901, Tarmac was patented, which held up much better to modern automobiles. Since this time, we have seen our road networks rapidly expand, with roads and highways growing both in size and in numbers.

How Self-Driving Cars Will Change Our Roads

Autonomous vehicles are no longer just a distant image in a utopian future. Google plans to complete its self-driving car by 2020. Audi, BMW, and Merecedes-Benz have all shown off their own driverless cars this year. Various other companies are working on their own versions of the concept as we speak. This being considered, it’s not too far-fetched to assume that driverless vehicles have the potential to entirely replace current automobiles within the next few decades.

[caption id=”attachment_1347” align=”aligncenter” width=”700”] Google’s latest self-driving car prototype. Source: Google[/caption]

As a matter of fact, Merecedes-Benz has projected that such vehicles will become commonplace by the year 2030. And the general public is becoming less skeptical of the whole idea in general. However, with such a fundamental change in the way we drive (or don’t drive, rather), there are sure to be some big changes to the roads we travel on as well.

The fundamental concept of roads will remain the same. That is, to get us from point A to point B in the most efficient way possible. The whole concept of efficiency changes when we are discussing computers instead of people, however.

Naturally, computers have the ability to do things much more efficiently than humans can. When the majority of the cars in use can drive themselves, human error will be a much lesser consideration when designing new roads and highways. This means that roads of the future can be more compact and economical with narrower lanes and higher speed limits, all while becoming safer in the process.

Narrower lanes

There’s no denying that wide lanes on roads are much more comfortable to drive on. With wide lanes, even shaky hands on the wheel probably won’t cause you to veer into the adjacent lane. This becomes totally irrelevant in a world with self-driving cars, though.

It’s easy to see why narrower lanes on city streets can be very beneficial in urban areas. When compared to 12-inch lanes, 10-inch lanes are safer and easier for pedestrians and bicyclists to cross. The extra space allows more room for sidewalks or protected bike lanes.

Compact highways

Most new highways and freeways being built nowadays are massive behemoths with wide medians and shoulders as well as room left over for future expansion. This isn’t even taking into account freeway interchanges; today, it’s not uncommon to see enormous four- and five-level stack interchanges being built on new and upgraded freeways in largely suburban areas.

When quirky human driving habits are taken out of the picture, though, there is less of a need for these kinds of freeway interchanges. While a stack interchange may be much more pleasant and safer to navigate for drivers, the computer in a self-driving car will have no qualms with, say, a cloverleaf interchange. At the very least, self-driving cars will be able to easily handle shorter on-ramps and off-ramps as well as narrower lanes and shoulders. Smaller highways and interchanges are cheaper to build and are environmentally preferable to larger ones.

[caption id=”attachment_1369” align=”aligncenter” width=”800”] With self-driving cars, massive highway interchanges may become a thing of the past. Image source[/caption]

Higher speed limits

Speed limits on roads are implemented for safety reasons. As speed increases, car accidents become exponentially more fatal. Because self-driving cars are inherently safer than their non-autonomous counterparts, higher speed limits should not be a problem. While computers may not be perfect, they can definitely drive in a much more uniform and consistent manner than people can.

The prospects become even greater once self-driving cars eliminate or dramatically reduce accidents caused by drunk or otherwise distracted driving. Additionally, drivers (or more accurately, passengers) will be able to talk or text in the car with no repercussions. The possibilities are endless.

There’s no way to tell what exactly a world with self-driving cars will eventually bring us. One thing is for sure, however–one day, roads will be built for computers, not for people.

A Quick Look at Bike Lanes

In many big cities around the world, designated bike lanes are becoming an increasingly common fixture on public roads. The concept of such lanes are simple enough—a marked lane is either striped off or built alongside a road or highway, separating bicycle traffic from motorized traffic. But are bike lanes always beneficial to local communities? Let’s take a look.

[caption id=”attachment_1330” align=”aligncenter” width=”600”] A bike lane in Arlington, Texas.[/caption]

Why do we need bike lanes?

In theory, bike lanes are constructed to ease travel for cyclists and to create a safer cycling environment. In urban areas with large amounts of combined automobile, pedestrian, and cycling traffic, separating transportation facilities for these groups of commuters generally makes travel more comfortable for everyone. Take an automobile driver, for example. Instead of having to worry about navigating a big, heavy car right behind a small and vulnerable bicycle, the driver can have the peace of mind of being able to drive alongside the cyclist instead. Similarly, the cyclist may take solace in not having to be sandwiched between two vehicles on the road.

Perhaps these concepts are even more true for bicycle lanes in suburban areas. Cycling on or alongside large, high-speed avenues can be downright terrifying for some, and installing bike lanes will undoubtedly help to ease these concerns.

Are bike lanes really safer?

This question is heavily disputed. There have been numerous studies on the subject, with results on opposite sides of the spectrum. Some studies, such as this one, show that streets with bicycle lanes on either side have either lower or similar injury rates for cyclists when compared to streets without such lanes. Meanwhile, other studies show that collision rates for cyclists traveling on sidewalks or bicycle paths are actually higher compared to cyclists traveling on the roadway itself.

There is no doubt that bicycle lanes add a certain level of complexity to intersections, particularly those with dedicated turn lanes for motorists. In such setups, the risks to both motorists and cyclists is greater because they must both cross paths. This type of configuration should only be implemented when warranted by a traffic study.

[caption id=”attachment_1314” align=”aligncenter” width=”250”] An example of an intersection with both a bike lane and dedicated right turn lane.[/caption]

Effects of bike lanes

Even though it may be the most heavily studied, safety isn’t the only aspect of bicycle lane implementation. A 2007 Copenhagen study showed that the installation of bicycle lanes led to a dramatic 18-20% increase in cycle traffic and a 9-10% decrease in car traffic on the reconstructed roads.

Increased bicycle use obviously has numerous indirect health benefits, stemming from both an increased level of physical exercise and a decrease in pollution from cars. It can be argued that the overall heath benefit of installing bicycle lanes is greater than the possible increase in traffic danger.

Are bike lanes worth it?

It depends. If the goal is to increase bicycle use and decrease motorized traffic, then yes, bicycle lanes are definitely effective. In an attempt to increase the safety of bicyclists, however, constructing bicycle lanes may not be the best way to go. Regardless, bicycle lanes have the ability to increase the happiness of cyclists as well as increase the health of the population overall when implemented properly.

Urban Sprawl, Its Effects, and Solutions

Urban sprawl is all around us—literally. Look around any big city and you’ll likely find miles upon miles of cramped big-box stores and single-family homes. Some commend this type of development, saying that it offers comfortable and affordable living to the masses. Others say that sprawl is simply human preference, and that there is nothing inherently wrong with it. What is undeniable, however, is the plethora of negative effects associated with urban sprawl. What exactly is urban sprawl, though?

What is urban sprawl?

[caption id=”attachment_1263” align=”aligncenter” width=”900”] Los Angeles sprawl. Source: Remi Jouan[/caption]

Urban sprawl is defined as the uncontrolled expansion of urban areas. Or as Wikipedia more aptly defines it, a multifaceted concept centered around the expansion of auto-oriented, low-density development. Sprawl occurs when people move away from urban city centers and into surrounding areas. This movement pattern generally results in previously-rural land turning into low-density, automobile-centric communities—otherwise known as the suburbs.

This is not a localized occurrence, either. While urban sprawl abroad may not be characterized by an overabundance of strip malls and Walmart stores like it is here in the United States, it is something that has significantly affected developed countries all around the world. For example, in the capital city of Canberra, Australia, it takes a whopping two and a half hours and 70 stops to travel from the southernmost suburbs to the northernmost suburbs by bus. Considering that Canberra has a modest population of under 400,000 people, those are not particularly admirable numbers.

While an affordable cost of entry and the promise of increased privacy and living are quite enticing, most potential homebuyers fail to consider the downfalls of owning a suburban home. A falsely idyllic image of suburban living is one of the reasons that low-density suburbs are so commonplace today, and can be an explanation as to why the expansion of urban sprawl shows no signs of slowing down anytime soon.

The effects of urban sprawl

While single-family homes can offer a more comfortable living environment for their residents, the benefits do not always outweigh the negatives. Here are some of the detrimental effects of urban sprawl:

Traffic

This is the obvious one. An abundance of low-density housing puts a tremendous strain on our roads and highways because most people in these communities drive their own personal vehicles to get to work. In most circumstances, this is not avoidable, because of the long commute distance and the complete lack of any sort of public transportation in many suburban cities. Road congestion can severely inhibit one’s ability to get anywhere, which can be catastrophic in emergency situations. Furthermore, road traffic costs billions of dollars per year in wasted fuel and lost productivity.

Cost of living

Suburban homes may be cheaper to purchase outright, but they are more expensive than their urban counterparts in nearly every other way. Specifically, residents of the suburbs pay much more for transportation and infrastructure. These individuals tend to live much further away from their workplaces than city-dwellers, and thus pay significantly more for fuel and vehicle maintenance. Additionally, the very nature of sprawl results in higher utility costs. Essentials such as water and electricity must be transported over longer distances, effectively raising the cost of such utilities. And when all of our tax dollars are focused on trying to sustain an unsustainable infrastructure, no one benefits.

Pollution

Suburban homes are almost universally more polluting than urban ones. As a matter of fact, the San Francisco suburb of Atherton has a per-household pollution score more than four times than that of downtown San Francisco. And judging by carbon footprint maps, similar trends can be found throughout the rest of the country.

Health

The car-based nature of suburban sprawl has undoubtedly suppressed our healthy habits—namely, walking. While urban areas are generally very pedestrian-friendly and have access to basic amenities within walking distance, the same can’t be said about the suburbs. A recent study has found that the risk of obesity and diabetes was elevated by 33 percent in areas of Toronto with poor walkability.

Solutions to urban sprawl

[caption id=”attachment_1265” align=”aligncenter” width=”1000”] Mixed-use buildings such as this one are often a component of planned communities. Source: Brett VA[/caption]

While sprawl has already spread to extreme lengths around the world, fortunately it can be stopped and its effects can be reversed. Doing so, however, will require cooperation from homebuyers, property developers, and government officials alike.

Sustainable development

While there are a sizable number of examples of planned communities and sustainable cities that have come into fruition over the past few decades, the continued development of sprawling subdivisions negate the benefits of these communities. This can be partially attributed to the modern-day bias towards urban sprawl; this is especially true of middle and upper class families, who largely prefer this type of living environment. If we are to halt the spread of urban sprawl, however, we must encourage the development of new urban communities along with educating the masses about the benefits of city living.

Government intervention

If private solutions to urban sprawl fail to materialize, government intervention may be required. In 1999, Al Gore proposed a plan to offer tax breaks to communities that preserve green space and find ways to relieve traffic congestion. While it may only be a small step in solving the bigger issue, offering such incentives may lead cities to encourage sustainable development over sprawling suburbs.

Even though the problem of urban sprawl shows no signs resolving itself anytime in the near future, if we can change the perception of the suburbs, we might be on our way to solving the issue. After all, the people are the main ones who can make a change when it comes to limiting suburban expansion. By spreading the word and refocusing our tax dollars, we can begin to solve even greater issues, such as pollution, health problems, and the crumbling infrastructure. Call me an optimist, but perhaps one day we can live in a world with no noticeable road congestion and minimal air pollution in our neighborhoods.

The Real Cost of Owning a Car

This article was originally published by The Urbanist. The Urbanist is a publication examining urban policy to improve cities. You can read their work here.

One of the biggest pleasures I get out of living in a city is that I don’t have to own a car. Cars can be a great convenience and a lot of people enjoy driving, but I’ve never been one of those people. I’ve always found driving stressful. Nevertheless, the conveniences of owning a car are huge. I still rent a car about once a month, carry memberships for car2go and Zipcar, and occasionally use taxis. With that said, my day to day life is possible without owning a car and I feel extremely fortunate to have this choice. This is entirely due to the way Seattle is designed but, unfortunately, not everyone can choose to get rid of their car.

Freedom to get from point A to point B is incredibly powerful, but providing more transit options is more empowering than simply giving people transportation choices. In fact, the most empowering aspect of transit is that it saves people a lot of money. I initially got rid of my car because I couldn’t afford it. The extra money was huge for me. In case you were wondering, here’s the cost of car ownership per year (from Consumer Reports), graphed against how long the car is owned.

That graph shows an average which might startle a lot of people; over $6,000 a year?! This is the truth though. The direct cost we incur for owning a car is huge. You might think to yourself, “That might be the average but I’m sure I pay way less.” Actually, you probably don’t pay much less. The article shows the cost for many cars. The lowest price is $5,000 a year:

[caption id=”attachment_1247” align=”aligncenter” width=”616”] See the list for all car types __here.[/caption]

This of course doesn’t take into consideration all the the other ways we pay for cars (like higher rents or taxes diverted to highways), but the direct costs are obvious. Not owning a car can save a ton of money and I’m glad I live in a city that give me this option.

What This Means For Housing

I pay more for rent than a lot of people. When I was looking for my apartment, I specifically looked in places that were centrally located and close to transit options. For my own situation, I’ve calculated that not owning a car allows me to pay about $300 per month more in rent. When we look at housing costs, it’s necessary to understand what other costs are required by the location of the housing. As an example, if your housing requires you to own a car, your housing is more expensive than it has to be.

The Most Dangerous Highways in America

Sure, we have all heard horror stories about the Death Road in Bolivia or avalanche-prone highways in China, but what about the roads here at home? With 4 million miles of road, the United States is bound to have some routes that are less-than-stellar when it comes to safety. Here is a look at some of the most dangerous highways in America, in no particular order.

I-15 from Los Angeles to Las Vegas

[caption id=”attachment_1187” align=”aligncenter” width=”800”] Interstate 15 in California. Source: Stan Shebs[/caption]

Sure, 180 miles of highway through the Mojave Desert is already ominous enough. It gets worse, though - a whopping total of 1,069 fatalities over a 15-year period makes it the most deadly stretch of road in the country. Clearly, this mostly four-lane highway cannot aptly handle the 8 million people that drive back and forth between Los Angeles and Las Vegas annually.

The road itself is not entirely to blame, though. Nearly half of those killed were not wearing seatbelts. Additionally, there have been numerous reports of deaths from drunk driving and high speeds. Many of these accidents could also likely be attributed to the surrounding conditions; dozens of miles of barren desert can cause drivers to lose focus and underestimate their speed.

U.S. Highway 431 in Alabama

[caption id=”attachment_1190” align=”aligncenter” width=”800”] Former alignment of U.S. 431 in Pittsview, Alabama.[/caption]

Appropriately nicknamed “Highway to Hell,” Alabama’s Highway 431 was once named as the country’s fourth most deadly road. Its high fatality rate is most likely attributable to the limited visibility of oncoming traffic and rapid transitions between two and four lanes of traffic, resulting in many head-on collisions.

However, the problem has been alleviated in recent years. After meeting the family of a fatal crash victim in 2003, the director of the Alabama Department of Transportation decided to expedite the construction of a bypass of the stretch of road which was most notable for its many vehicular fatalities.

U.S. Route 550 in Colorado

[caption id=”attachment_1191” align=”aligncenter” width=”800”] Million Dollar Highway in Colorado. Source: AFBorchert[/caption]

Perhaps the most scenic of all the highways on this list, a portion of U.S. Route 550 in Colorado known as the Million Dollar Highway is known for its hairpin turns, steep cliffs, and narrow lanes. Oh, and a lack of guardrails. This stretch travels through a gorge and is cut directly into the mountain in many places.

Many thrill-seekers travel on this road because of the inherent risk involved. The lack of a shoulder throughout most of this section means that weaving out of the main lane will send you plunging directly off of the mountain - which reaches heights of up to 11,000 feet above sea level.

California State Route 138

[caption id=”attachment_1192” align=”aligncenter” width=”800”] California State Route 138. Source: AARoads[/caption]

California State Route 138 has been the site of many serious car accidents over recent years, no doubt thanks to its twisting, mountainous path and undivided two-lane status - especially in the overloaded eastern portion of the route. Locally, this road is known as Deathtrap Highway and “The Widowmaker.”

One particularly chilling chain of events occurred in 2003, when a mother’s pickup truck swerved off the highway and plunged into the California Aqueduct - an accident which killed her and her three children. Relatives of the family were awarded $10 million from the state of California when it was determined that the accident could have been prevented if the state had installed a $26,000 guardrail extension.

Dalton Highway in Alaska

[caption id=”attachment_1193” align=”aligncenter” width=”800”] Dalton Highway in Brooks Range, Alaska.[/caption]

If it’s on Ice Road Truckers, you know it’s going to be a tough route to travel. There are only three towns along the 414 mile highway, each with a population of barely a dozen permanent residents. On one 240-mile stretch, there are no gas stations, restaurants, hotels, or any other basic services, and there are no medical facilities on the entire highway. The Arctic climate can lead to questionable road conditions, and many portions of the highway are covered in potholes and craters.

Understandably, most of the traffic on the Dalton Highway consists of trucks carrying oil and supplies. Traveling in a small car or on a motorcycle can be quite dangerous, especially due to the numerous gravel-only portions of the road. For those that do decide to traverse it, however, the breathtaking views are truly something to behold.

While this list covers some of the most dangerous highways in America, by no means is it comprehensive. If there’s any roads that I left out, let me know in the comments, and I just might add them.

How Self-Driving Cars are the Future

It’s no secret that companies such as Google and Volvo have been testing their own self-driving cars for quite some time now. These vehicles aren’t just confined to test tracks and no-name roads, either. Not only have Google’s cars traveled across the Golden Gate Bridge, but they have also successfully traversed the city traffic and hairpin turns of San Francisco’s Lombard Street. Meanwhile, 100 of Volvo’s autonomous cars have been driving along test routes in Gothenburg, Sweden as of this month. Do self-driving cars have a place in our everyday lives, though? My answer is yes, definitely.

There’s still a lot of work to be done before you’ll be able to find a driverless car at your local dealership, however. In the US, only four states have legalized the testing of such cars on public roads: Nevada, California, Florida, and Michigan. Furthermore, Google’s self-driving cars have only mapped 2,000 miles of road; the United States has a whopping total of 4 million miles of roadways. These maps don’t consist of just a simple layout of highways and stop signs, either. A self-driving car must be completely aware of its surroundings, and this includes knowing the exact position of every curb and the height of every traffic signal. While creating such a map of an entire country seems like a daunting task, the implication of having this type of system in place would be enormous.

[caption id=”attachment_1147” align=”aligncenter” width=”768”] Lombard Street. Google’s self-driving car drove through here on its own.[/caption]

Even though naysayers argue against self-driving cars due concerns over damage liability and a loss of jobs, the large host of potential benefits offered by these vehicles make their widespread implementation inevitable. Some benefits of an autonomous car include:

  • Fewer accidents and collisions. Theoretically, a computer-controlled vehicle would have quicker response times and a greater knowledge of its surroundings, making the road a much safer place.
  • Decreased traffic congestion. An elimination of human error and a reduced need for safety gaps will increase road capacity and shrink travel times.
  • Easier overnight travel. With the car driving itself, its passengers can stay well-rested during long road trips.

  • No need for redundant passengers. Without the need for a human driver, self-driving cars can pick up passengers on their own.
    A idyllic society with no human drivers and only self-driving cars would offer even more benefits, including but not limited to:

  • Higher overall speed limits. With self-driving cars behind safer than human drivers, speed limits could harmlessly be increased.

  • Reduction of insurance rates. With safe autonomous driving, the need for auto insurance can be reduced or eliminated altogether.
  • Smaller need for traffic police. Self-driving cars will automatically abide by traffic laws and regulations.
    No matter how great all of these things may sound, it could still be many years before a truly autonomous car is available to the public. Google has stated that they would like to have some form of self-driving cars on the road by 2018, but many legal hurdles may have to be overcome before such cars will be readily available for purchase by the average consumer. However, a close look at the modern automobile industry reveals that cars are actually becoming more and more autonomous as time goes on. For example, many new vehicles offer self-braking technology which can automatically stop the car whenever a crash is anticipated. By the end of the year, Volvo plans to release a feature called Adaptive Cruise Control, which automatically follows the vehicle ahead in queues. Similarly, many other automakers have announced plans for implementing autonomous features in their cars. By the 2020s, many leading automobile manufacturers plan to have vehicles on the market which can drive themselves at least part of the time.

[caption id=”attachment_1150” align=”aligncenter” width=”768”] The 2015 Volvo XC90 will offer a feature called Adaptive Cruise Control in which the car ahead of it is followed.[/caption]

No matter how long it takes for the technology to get to the market, self-driving cars will still continue to be an exciting and imminent prospect for years to come. It should be interesting to see how the technology plays out among lawmakers and consumers.

The Hidden Facts About the Suburbs

For many people, the image of a perfect life consists of owning a modest home within the suburbs and living happily with their families. Over the past few decades, we have seen a shift in living preferences; while living in dense city centers was once a common preference, the widespread implementation of low-cost single-family housing has led to changing ideals for the typical American family. In modern-day societies, many potential home buyers see the suburbs as being inherently advantageous, and some will even fail to consider purchasing an urban home. However, the suburbs do bring along a plethora of negative effects - a list of which I have complied below.

  1. Suburban expansion leads to urban sprawl. This effect is perhaps not-so-hidden. It is widely known that the development of suburbs is a direct component of urban sprawl, which brings along with it an entire host of effects - namely, increased traffic congestion and transportation costs.
  2. Suburban homes are far more polluting than their urban counterparts. According to research from the University of California, Berkeley, the predominantly suburban Bay Area city of Atherton , California has a per-household pollution score that is more than four times that of downtown San Francisco. Interestingly, high-wealth suburban areas tend to be the worst offenders, with this carbon footprint map showing that city centers and poorer areas have a much lower carbon footprint than wealthy suburbs of urban areas.
  3. The suburbs are causing infrastructure costs to rise. In low-density suburban areas, roads and utilities must be stretched over a greater distance. This results in higher costs to the taxpayer, offsetting the savings from buying a suburban home. The City of Edmonton has determined that over the next 30 years, suburban sprawl will actually cost taxpayers more than the expansion will provide in revenues.
  4. The suburbs are hurting our health. Most Americans are aware that obesity is becoming an increasingly prominent issue in the United States. This is generally attributed to our unhealthy eating habits and increasingly sedentary lifestyles. However, people generally fail to make the connection between their residences and their health. Suburban neighborhoods are often lacking in walkability, and most residents of these neighborhoods rely on personal vehicles to get to where they need to go. These factors lead to these health problems described above.
    Of course, the suburbs also offer positive benefits, such as a lower cost of entry and an increase of privacy. For some, these factors may outweigh the negatives. But for others, knowing the entire truth may affect their home-buying decision. Ultimately, I would suggest extensively researching the pros and cons of each type of living environment if or when you are considering buying a new home.

The True Cost of Bad Roads

$1 to maintain, $7 to rebuild.

That’s what the director of the Michigan Department of Transportation says, at least. By this logic, it costs a dollar to keep a road well-maintained for every seven dollars you would have to spend to rebuild it entirely. And according to the superfluously-named American Association of State Highway and Transportation Officials, the cost of rebuilding a road after 25 years of neglect can be more than triple the cost to keep the road in good condition in the first place. However, we live in a society that tends to place more emphasis on major construction and redevelopment projects than necessary continual maintenance. And the implications of such a society are enormous.

By keeping the road maintenance budget low, the cost to taxpayers is actually _increased _in a variety of ways. In order to pay for the comparatively high cost of reconstructing a road entirely, in many cases, a city will either temporarily or permanently increase tax rates, which will ultimately cost taxpayers more in the long run. On the other hand, simply not repairing these roads at all will also hurt the wallets of taxpayers, who will have to deal with increased vehicle wear and repair bills.

The cost of bad roads to drivers is significantly variable depending on the locality. On average, each American motorist will pay $335 per year for additional operating costs and vehicle repairs alone. This figure is significantly higher in some parts of the country, with the average New Jersey driver paying $596 more in repair bills each year, for example. Meanwhile, Georgia drivers are only paying an additional $44 per year in repair costs, on average.

[caption id=”attachment_998” align=”aligncenter” width=”1024”] Crumbling curbs, uneven pavement, and potholes are all characteristic of bad roads.[/caption]

That’s just the surface of the issue, though. The figures above fail to take into account the costs of lost time and revenues to American workers and businesses. If we are to listen to the warnings of President Obama and the American Society of Civil Engineers, the situation will become dire very soon. According to the ASCE, if $1.1 trillion more isn’t spent on the nation’s infrastructure by the year 2020, it will cost the country nearly 3.5 million jobs and $1 trillion per year in lost business sales. Researchers at Texas A&M University project that between now and 2035, rough roads and congestion will cost the average Texas household $6,100 per year unless spending increases.

However, some say that the situation is not nearly as bad as it is made out to be. Ron Utt, a transportation consultant of the Reagan administration, is okay with increased spending on roads in certain congested areas. Still, Utt says that the rather shocking numbers given by the ASCE is what would be required to bring all of the country’s roads up to perfect condition, which is far from necessary.

Personally, I think that some of this money should be used for implementing other transportation solutions to complement our already-extensive road system, such as high-speed rail and pedestrian walkways.

Is a Car-Free City a Practical Idea?

For many of us, it’s nearly impossible to imagine a life without killer rush hours, incessant and problematic road construction, and seemingly endless traffic issues. However distant a world without these elements may seem, though, there was once a time when none of these problems existed. It’s hard to believe that barely more than a century ago, personal automobiles were nothing more than a dream for the average individual.

That all changed in 1908, of course, when Henry Ford’s assembly line opened the door for the luxury of unrestrained travel to millions of middle-class Americans. Understandably, the Ford Model T was a huge success. In the words of Henry Ford himself, the Ford Model T was “so low in price that no man making a good salary will be unable to own one – and enjoy with his family the blessing of hours of pleasure in God’s great open spaces.” Now what doesn’t sound appealing about that?

The personal automobile has transformed the world since then - and that’s an understatement. Cars are both a blessing and a curse in our modern-day society. According to a 2011 article, the world’s car population has surpassed one billion, with the amount of cars worldwide to increase to 2.5 billion by the year 2050. I’m not going to go into a big argument about scarcity of resources and greenhouse gases here, but surely billions of vehicles pumping CO2 into our atmosphere can’t possibly be a good thing…right? Not to mention that the world requires almost 100 million barrels of oil per day, a statistic which is a bit concerning considering that this oil is not an infinite resource.

[caption id=”attachment_1241” align=”aligncenter” width=”1000”] Oil production and consumption has increased on every continent in recent years.[/caption]

While electric vehicles offer a potential solution to the problem of limited resources and pollution, they do nothing to resolve the world’s other crippling issue with personal transportation - traffic. If you commute by car in a large metropolitan area, rush hour traffic is generally unavoidable. There’s not really a cure for this traffic, either, especially in growing areas. Expanding roads provides temporary relief, but actually makes congestion worse in the long run - a phenomenon which I briefly described in a prior post. To make matters worse, road expansions make conditions much more unpleasant during the duration of the construction itself.

According to this infographic by Nationwide, the average yearly cost of road congestion to each driver is $713, which is more than an entire week’s worth of wages for the average American.

The obvious alternative is the widespread implementation and usage of public transportation. However, it’s not nearly as simple as it sounds. Numerous studies have shown that people are irrationally biased towards automobiles, and many people will choose to drive their cars even in situations where public transportation is proven to be quicker and more economically feasible.

This behavior alone would make the development of a successful car-free city a difficult task, especially in the current-day. However, China is attempting it. Outside of Chengdu, 78 million square feet of land has been set aside for the construction of a new city designed to house 80,000 residents, none of whom will need a car to get around. In this so-called “Great City,” transportation will be provided by electric shuttles, and all residences will be within a two-minute walk of a public park. The city also plans to implement eco-friendly features in order to save energy and minimize waste. However, we have yet to see if this will be successful in China, and whether or not such a development could be successfully built elsewhere. Still, it’s an impressive plan.

[caption id=”attachment_954” align=”aligncenter” width=”800”] China’s “Great City” will house 80,000 people and be entirely navigable by foot and electric shuttle. Image source: Adrian Smith + Gordon Gill Architecture[/caption]

While large cities with no cars are basically nonexistent in today’s times, there are a few examples of cities with significant pedestrian-only areas. Freiburg, Germany is a good example of this - Freiburg has both a large pedestrian zone and an extensive public transport system.

Perhaps a more relatable example of a mostly car-free community is the average modern-day university. Even though it’s not entirely comparable to an actual city, many large universities offer education, jobs, housing, and dining all in one constrained space - which is completely accessible by foot or bicycle.

Only time will tell if an entirely car-free city is a current-day possibility or merely an element of an unrealistic utopia. One thing is for sure, however - the world’s irrational aversion to public transit has to come to an end before anything like this can become a widespread part of our future. Once this happens, our future generations just might see the day when no one needs a car to get to where they need to be.
